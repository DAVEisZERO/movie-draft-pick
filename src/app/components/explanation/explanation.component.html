How does this work? By using the FART selection process:
1. Fetch the movies. Look for the link icon.
Create or use a public movie list on Letterboxd, for example the 100 highest-rated animated movies. Copy the link to the list and submit it in this app.
2. Add viewers. Look for the edit icon.
Add the people who will help you choose the movie. Each person gets a different color so it's easy to see who's who.
3. Review and ban movies.
In rounds, have each person ban some movies they don't want to watch by clicking their color and then the movie. The numbers next to the viewers' names indicate the number of bans per viewer to ensure that there are at least three movies left, but you can ban as many as you like.
4. Then pick a winner. Look for the dice icon.
Assuming there is more than one movie left, choose the final movie from the remaining options. The app itself doesn't have a way to pick the winner, but suggests using an impartial method like spinning a roulette wheel.

 Why use the FART selection process for picking a movie?

Choosing a movie to watch with a group can often lead to disagreements and frustration. The FART selection process offers a unique and effective way to ensure everyone's voice is heard while making the selection process fun and fair. The main benefits are:

  • Balanced Input: each viewer has the power to veto movies they strongly dislike, ensuring no one is stuck watching a movie they really don't want to see.
  • Fair Elimination: banning movies in rounds allows for a gradual narrowing down of choices, giving everyone a chance to influence the decision multiple times.
  • Interactive and Fun: the process is engaging and can be more enjoyable than simply voting or being told what to watch.
  • Final Choice Variety: ensures a minimum of three movies remain, providing options and preventing a single dominant choice.
  • Creative Final Decision: the suggestion to use a random method like a roulette adds an element of surprise and excitement to the final selection.

Unlike democratic votes or authoritarian decisions, this method ensures that everyone's preferences are considered, reduces the chance of dissatisfaction, and makes the movie-picking process an enjoyable part of the group activity.

The birth of the FART selection process

Once upon a time, in a bustling city, there was a close-knit group of friends who loved to watch movies together. This group, known as the "Film Buffs", had a tradition of gathering every Friday night for a movie marathon. However, what should have been a fun and relaxing event often turned into a source of tension and frustration.

Each week, the Film Buffs faced the same problem: deciding which movie to watch. They tried different methods, but none seemed to work. When they voted democratically, the same genres or directors always won, leaving some members feeling left out. On the rare occasions when they let one person decide, it caused resentment and complaints from the rest. They needed a better way.

One particularly heated Friday night, after yet another argument over which movie to watch, a member named Billy had a lightbulb moment. Billy, a software developer by profession, realized that their problem wasn't unique and that technology might offer a solution. Over the weekend, Billy started working on a new system, something that would be fair and inclusive but also fun.

Billy called the new system the "Fair and Random Tiebreaker", or FART for short. The idea was simple but ingenious.

  1. Users would start by creating or selecting a movie list on Letterboxd, a popular film database. This way, everyone could see the options and add their suggestions.
  2. Each person involved in the movie night would be added as a viewer in the FART app. They would each be assigned a different color to represent their choices, making the process visually engaging.
  3. Instead of voting directly for their favorites, viewers would take turns banning movies they didn't want to watch. This would be done in rounds, ensuring everyone had a chance to veto a few choices without eliminating all the options too quickly.
  4. After several rounds, they would be left with a few remaining movies. The final step would involve selecting the winner using a fun, random method like a roulette wheel, adding an element of surprise and excitement to the decision.

Billy presented the FART process to the Film Buffs the next Friday. At first, there were giggles about the name, but everyone agreed to give it a try. They created a list of movies on Letterboxd, added each member as a viewer, and started the banning process. What happened next was nothing short of magical.

For the first time, everyone felt involved and respected in the decision-making process. The rounds of banning movies turned into a fun game, with laughter and light-hearted banter replacing the usual arguments. When it came time to pick the final movie using the roulette, the group held their breath in anticipation, and the excitement made the viewing experience even better.

Word of the FART process spread quickly. Other friend groups, families, and even clubs started adopting the method for their movie nights. What began as a solution to the Film Buffs' Friday night dilemma turned into a popular way for people everywhere to pick movies fairly and enjoyably.

Billy, seeing how well the FART process worked, developed it into a full-fledged app, making it easier for anyone to use. The Film Buffs continued their tradition, now with a system that guaranteed everyone had a voice, and their movie nights became more enjoyable than ever.

And so, the FART selection process was born out of a need for fairness and fun, transforming movie nights and ensuring that everyone had a say in the cinematic adventures they embarked on together.
